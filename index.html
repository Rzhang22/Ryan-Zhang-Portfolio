Precision machining work supporting the electric drivetrain of the RFR Formula SAE car. Focused on manufacturing custom jigs and fixtures critical for correct powertrain alignment and chassis integration.
Accumulator Alignment
Precision Machining
Machined high-precision jigs using a lathe to properly align the accumulator to the chassis — a critical step for safe and reliable electric powertrain integration.
Jig accuracy directly affects accumulator position relative to the chassis, impacting weight distribution, structural integrity, and electrical safety clearances.
Jig geometry developed from SolidWorks reference model — critical dimensions transferred to setup sheet and verified with calipers at each machining stage to ensure final part matched design intent within tolerance.
Accumulator alignment directly governs high-voltage connector engagement and pack compressive preload; misalignment outside tolerance risked both structural and electrical failure modes, making jig precision a safety-critical requirement.
Manufacturing Process
Lathe · Fixtures
Operated a manual lathe to achieve tight tolerances required for race-car-grade components where misalignment has direct performance and safety consequences.
Collaborated with the powertrain and chassis sub-teams to validate fit and function of machined parts within the full vehicle assembly.
Machining operations sequenced to minimize setup changes — facing, turning, and boring completed in a single chuck setup where possible to preserve concentricity and reduce cumulative positional error.
Parts inspected against engineering drawings post-machining using calipers and go/no-go gauges; any out-of-tolerance features flagged and disposition determined in coordination with the powertrain lead before assembly.

Vehicle Dynamics · Rutgers Formula Racing
Steering Weight
Reduction
162.99 N → 110.31 N · 32.3% Reduction in Driver Effort
Full analytical investigation into steering effort sources on a Formula SAE electric race car. Developed MATLAB models quantifying both friction and suspension geometry contributions, then implemented a geometry modification achieving a 32.3% reduction in steering force without altering tire grip or self-aligning torque characteristics.
Tire Friction Model
MATLAB · Force Analysis
Identified tire-ground friction as the dominant steering load source. Racing slicks (μ ≈ 0.8) generate substantially higher frictional torque at the contact patch than road tires, feeding directly into the steering column via the upright and tie rod.
Derived force path analytically: F_friction = μ · M_corner · g → T_ground = F_f · r_scrub → F_tierod = T_g / d_tierod → F_rack = F_t × 2 → T_pinion = F_r · r_pinion → F_wheel = T_pinion / r_steer. Implemented in MATLAB with parametric inputs for scrub radius, tie rod length, rack ratio, and steering wheel radius.
Key parameters: m = 318.15 kg, F_bias = 47.77% front, r_scrub = 44.96 mm, r_steer = 123.83 mm, d_tierod = 23.13 mm, r_pinion = 7.94 mm.
Suspension Geometry Model
KPI · Caster · MATLAB
Modeled the steering effort contribution from Kingpin Inclination Angle (KPI = 0.099 rad) and Caster (0.087 rad). During cornering, these angles cause the chassis to physically lift and lower — requiring the driver to work against vehicle weight in addition to tire friction.
Height gain equations: h_KPI = r_scrub · sin(KPI) · δ and h_Caster = trail · sin(Caster) · δ, where δ = steering lock angle (0.314 rad). Total lift h_t = h_KPI + h_Caster; work W = F_front · h_t; converted to steering force via rack and wheel ratios.
Combined friction and suspension models give full driver effort at any steering angle. Ackermann geometry noted as a model limitation — inner/outer wheels trace different radii, making absolute force values less meaningful than percent differences between configurations.
Design Trade-off Analysis
Caster · KPI · Geometry
Evaluated three potential reduction strategies: (1) reduce Caster — lowers lift-induced effort and reduces negative camber gain on the outside wheel, but reduces cornering grip; (2) increase KPI — decreases scrub radius and friction torque, but increases self-aligning torque making steering heavier at low speeds; (3) increase mechanical advantage — no effect on tire behavior.
Caster and KPI modifications were rejected as both create unacceptable handling compromises — self-aligning torque must be maintained within a calibrated range to preserve driver feedback. Too little torque produces numb, unpredictable steering; too much reintroduces the original problem.
Concluded that increasing the tie rod lever arm was the only modification that reduced effort without altering suspension kinematics, camber curves, or self-aligning torque characteristics.
Implementation & Result
32.3% · 162.99 N → 110.31 N
Solution: translated the steering rack forward ½ in and the tie rod upright mounting point forward ½ in simultaneously. This increases the perpendicular distance from the kingpin axis to the tie rod line of action — effectively lengthening the lever arm the driver has over the tire contact patch.
All suspension geometry (caster, KPI, camber curves, toe), self-aligning torque, and Ackermann behavior remain unchanged. The only kinematic consequence is a marginal reduction in maximum achievable steering angle, which was evaluated against the vehicle's minimum turning radius requirement and confirmed acceptable.
Measured outcome: driver steering effort reduced from 162.99 N to 110.31 N — a 32.3% decrease. Reduction verified by MATLAB model comparison between original and modified tie rod geometry configurations. Physical validation ongoing through track testing.

Aerodynamics · RFR
CFD
Simulations
ANSYS Fluent · Cradle CFD
High-fidelity CFD simulations on the full aerodynamic package — front wing, rear wing, and diffuser — to evaluate downforce, drag, and flow behavior for performance optimization.
Simulation Setup
CFD
Developed parametric 3D CAD models of each aero component and generated high-quality structured and unstructured meshes for turbulent, incompressible flow.
Utilized turbulence models including k-ε and k-ω SST to capture flow separation, vortex generation, and boundary layer behavior.
Mesh refinement applied at leading edges, trailing edges, and near-wall regions using inflation layers to resolve the boundary layer velocity gradient accurately — y⁺ values maintained within the valid range for each turbulence model.
Simulation domain sized with inlet, outlet, and symmetry boundary conditions set to replicate open-road freestream conditions. Convergence monitored via residual plots and force coefficient stabilization across iterations.
Analysis
Downforce + Drag
Analyzed downforce, drag, and pressure distribution under varying angles of attack and ride heights.
Investigated front/rear aero interaction with the diffuser for vehicle balance, ground effect, and underbody flow acceleration.
Calculated lift-to-drag ratios, identified flow recirculation zones, and validated design iterations to propose geometry modifications.
Compared pressure coefficient distributions across multiple wing configurations to isolate the contribution of endplate geometry and flap angle on total downforce — used to justify final aero package selection.
Post-processed velocity and turbulence kinetic energy fields to identify wake interaction between front and rear wing elements, informing ride height and rake angle adjustments to reduce aerodynamic interference drag.

Smart Walking
Cane
Spring 2026 · 10 Prototypes · ASA Final Production
End-to-end design and manufacture of an obstacle-detection walking cane that outperforms commercial competitors on all key metrics: 15% longer battery life, 10% further detection range, 25% of the cost, at only 100g additional weight. Role encompassed problem definition, DFM-driven CAD, material selection, iterative 3D printing, and tolerance refinement across 10 full prototype cycles.
Problem Definition
Requirements Engineering
Traditional canes rely entirely on physical contact for obstacle detection — table surfaces, hanging AC units, and elevated hazards go undetected until impact, representing a critical safety gap for visually impaired users.
Competitive benchmarking established target specifications: 15% longer battery life, 10% further detection range, 25% of competitor cost, weight penalty ≤ 100g. All four targets met in final design.
System-level architecture defined to integrate ultrasonic/IR sensor array, microcontroller, power management circuit, and haptic/audio feedback within a housing attachable to standard cane handles without permanent modification.
Housing Design & DFM
SolidWorks · GD&T · DFM
Housing designed to minimum footprint while accommodating full electronics assembly with accessible wire routing for field repair. Internal geometry optimized around PCB envelope, battery cell dimensions, and connector clearances.
Material selection: ASA chosen over PLA and PETG for UV resistance (outdoor use), moisture resistance, chemical resistance, and favorable strength-to-cost ratio for final production shells. PLA used exclusively for prototype iterations to reduce cost and cycle time.
Sensor housing geometry required vertical shell orientation during FDM printing, placing layer lines transverse to primary load direction — identified as a structural failure point. Resolved through internal pocket redesign redistributing load away from layer interfaces.
Tolerance & Print Iteration
FDM · ±0.1mm · 10 Prototypes
Completed 10 full prototype cycles at 6-hour print durations per cycle in PLA, systematically varying wall thickness, infill density, and print orientation to characterize dimensional variance across builds.
Part clearances refined to ±0.1 mm after empirical measurement of print variance — critical for snap-fit closure, sensor port alignment, and button actuator travel distance. Clearance values tuned per feature type based on measured printer repeatability.
Rounded hinge geometry initially produced support-dependent surfaces with unacceptable surface finish and dimensional inaccuracy at the hinge bore. Redesigned to 45° chamfers, eliminating support structures entirely while maintaining structural cross-section and achieving print-in-place tolerances.
Hardware & Software Integration
Embedded Systems · Active
Currently manufacturing and assembling final production units in ASA. Internal hardware integration ongoing — routing, securing, and connecting sensor array, microcontroller, and power management components within the finalized housing geometry.
Embedded software under active optimization for detection algorithm response latency, false-positive filtering, and power consumption profiling to validate the 15% battery life improvement target against measured draw under field operating conditions.
End-to-end ownership of the project: problem definition → system architecture → mechanical design → DFM iteration → tolerance validation → hardware assembly → software tuning. No team — sole designer and manufacturing lead throughout.

Independent Project · Design Lead
Electric
Chainsaw
Fall 2023 · 50+ Components · Full CAD Assembly
Led a team in developing a complete parametric CAD model of an electric chainsaw — 50+ components spanning external housing, internal drive mechanism, cooling system, and gear reduction train. Design process balanced ergonomics, packaging constraints, motor thermal management, and operator safety.
Housing & Ergonomics
SolidWorks · Industrial Design
Designed the external housing with ergonomics and operator utility as primary constraints. Handle geometry and grip angle determined by two-handed operational posture to minimize fatigue during extended use.
Incorporated a clear polycarbonate hand guard positioned to maintain full sightlines to the bar and chain during cutting — a direct safety requirement driven by operator visibility analysis.
Protruding foot geometry on the housing underside ensures the chain bar is always elevated off a flat surface when the tool is set down — preventing contact damage to the chain and eliminating a common kick-start hazard.
Housing packaging optimized around motor envelope, battery cell dimensions, and chain tensioner access — internal volume allocated in priority order: motor cooling path first, then battery, then electronics, with all service access points designed for tool-free entry.
Drive Mechanism & Gear Train
Gear Reduction · Thermal Management
Motor selection and placement optimized to maximize motor diameter within the housing cross-section — larger motor volume directly improves continuous torque output and reduces thermal load per unit length of stator.
Motor output shaft drives an integrated centrifugal cooling fan directly — no parasitic belt or coupling losses. Fan geometry sized to maintain adequate airflow over the motor end windings at operating RPM range.
Spur gear set steps down motor speed to match chain sprocket requirements. Gear ratio selection balanced between chain speed (cutting performance) and torque multiplication (load handling) — ratio finalized through chain speed and cutting force calculations.
Complete drivetrain modeled as a full parametric assembly: motor shaft → fan hub → pinion gear → driven gear → clutch drum → sprocket → chain. Each interface modeled with correct mating constraints and clearances to validate no interference across the operating envelope.
